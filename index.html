Getting started

Where to start a smart home without wasting money

Most people over-buy in their first month — a hub, three types of bulbs, and a lock, all from different ecosystems that barely talk to each other. The better approach is to fix one daily annoyance first: a light you always forget to turn off, a thermostat you keep adjusting manually, a door you're never sure you locked.

Start with a single smart plug or bulb in the room you use most. It's inexpensive, works with nearly every ecosystem, and teaches you how the app, the automations, and the voice commands actually feel before you commit to anything bigger.

Buyer's guide

WiFi, Zigbee, or Matter — does the protocol actually matter?

Short answer: only if you plan to own more than a handful of devices. WiFi devices are the simplest to set up and need no extra hardware, but they can crowd your router as you add more. Zigbee devices route through a separate hub and tend to be more reliable at scale, which matters once you're past five or six gadgets.

Matter is the newer standard built to make all of this work together regardless of brand — look for the Matter logo if you want some insurance against getting locked into one ecosystem.

Honest take

Five signs a "smart" gadget isn't actually worth it

Not everything needs an app. A genuinely useful smart device usually solves a scheduling problem, a safety concern, or a repetitive task — not just adds a screen to something that worked fine before. Before buying, ask what you'll actually do differently once it's connected.

If the honest answer is "check an app instead of glancing at it," it's probably not worth the price premium over the non-smart version.
